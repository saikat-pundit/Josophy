### 1. MACROECONOMIC, INFLATION EXPECTATIONS & LIQUIDITY TRENDS

The US economy has entered a phase of **"Sticky-Long" normalization**. While headline GDP growth remains robust at a **4.56% 2-Year CAGR**, the momentum is decelerating, with QoQ growth flatlining at 0.00%. This suggests the economy is hitting a ceiling imposed by restrictive financial conditions rather than collapsing into recession.

The critical divergence lies in the inflation narrative. Actual CPI inflation has surged to **+3.90% YoY**, significantly outpacing the **2-Year CAGR of +3.29%**. More alarmingly, short-term inflation expectations have detached from the Fed's target. The **1-Year Expected Inflation (EXPINF1YR)** has jumped to **3.02%**, accelerating sharply from its 2-Year annualized trend of +23 bps. Liquidity conditions are tightening structurally. While M2 Money Supply is expanding at a healthy **+4.60% CAGR**, the Fed Balance Sheet has contracted by **-3.50% CAGR** over the last two years. This quantitative tightening (QT) is acting as a silent brake, absorbing the liquidity generated by fiscal expansion (Federal Debt up **+5.90% CAGR**). The result is a market where cash is plentiful in the banking system but expensive to deploy, creating a friction that suppresses velocity without triggering a liquidity crisis.

**Key Takeaway:** The macro backdrop is defined by a clash between resilient real growth and re-accelerating short-term inflation expectations, forcing the market to price out imminent rate cuts.

*   **Medium-End (10Y):** The **10Y Yield** is the anchor of stability, rising a modest **+6 bps annualized** over two years to 4.49%. The **30Y-2Y Spread** (Global Liquidity Proxy) is **+84 bps**, indicating that long-term investors are demanding a significant premium to hold duration amidst fiscal uncertainty and sticky inflation expectations.

However, this is being eroded by **Actual CPI inflation at 3.90% YoY**. For the first time in this cycle, nominal wage growth is lagging immediate price increases, resulting in **negative real wage growth** on a trailing 12-month basis.

The psychological impact is evident in the **Inflation Expectations Nexus**. While long-term expectations (10Y) remain anchored at 2.49%, the **1-Year Expected Inflation has surged to 3.02%**. This indicates that Main Street does not believe the inflation battle is won; consumers are bracing for higher prices in the immediate future. This fear is altering behavior: despite a low **Unemployment Rate of 4.20%** (only +10 bps over 2 years), the **Housing Supply** has surged **+12.08% CAGR**. This isn't just new construction; it reflects a freeze in existing home sales. Homeowners with locked-in low mortgage rates (implied by the current **10Y Yield of 4.49%** and **30Y at 4.98%**) are refusing to sell, while potential buyers are priced out by the combination of high yields and stagnant real wages.

**Conclusion:** The consumer is tired. The buffer of pandemic savings is depleted, and with real purchasing power shrinking, the resilience of the **4.56% GDP CAGR** is increasingly dependent on corporate investment and government spending, not household consumption. Watch the **1Y Inflation Expectation** closely; if it crosses 3.25%, wage-price spiral fears will dominate the narrative, forcing a more aggressive Fed stance that could finally crack the labor market.
